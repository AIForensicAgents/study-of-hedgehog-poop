# study-of-hedgehog-poop

A friendly, professional repository documenting a biological and educational study of hedgehog scat (droppings, feces). This project collects morphology notes, habitat/diet clues, and curated imagery to support field identification, monitoring, and classroom activities — all with an emphasis on ethics and safety.

---

## Overview

This repository is designed for naturalists, students, field biologists, and citizen scientists interested in learning how hedgehog droppings can inform ecological and behavioral studies. Contents include:

- High-quality photographic examples of hedgehog feces across habitats.
- Field morphology notes and quick ID cues.
- Guidance on interpreting diet and habitat signals from droppings.
- Ethics and safety best practices for handling/photographing samples.
- A growing image gallery for education and reference.

Intended purpose: **educational and non-invasive research**. This is not a guide for capturing or harming wildlife — it is for observation, documentation, and respectful science.

---

## Why it matters

Understanding hedgehog scat is valuable because:

- It helps monitor population presence and activity patterns without trapping or direct handling.
- Diet information (from droppings) reveals food availability, seasonal shifts, and potential threats (pesticide exposure, lack of prey).
- Non-invasive sampling supports conservation assessments and urban ecology studies.
- Educationally, scat studies teach students about trophic ecology, decomposition, and field survey methods.

In short: hedgehog scat is a low-impact, information-rich data source for wildlife monitoring and environmental education.

## Morphology notes

Common morphological features to observe and record (use calipers or a small ruler for scale in photos):

- **Size**: Hedgehog droppings typically range from ~0.5–2.5 cm in length depending on diet and individual. Measure length and width.
- **Shape**: Often cylindrical, slightly tapered at ends; may appear segmented or smooth.
- **Color**: Varies with diet — dark brown to black (insectivorous), lighter browns when fruit/plant material present.
- **Texture**: Can be firm and dry (insect-rich diet) or softer/more friable (high vegetable/berry content).
- **Contents**: Look for undigested insect exoskeleton fragments, hairs, seeds, slugs/snail remains (radula fragments are rare but possible), and plant fibers.
- **Coatings & parasites**: Mucous coatings may indicate digestive upset; visible parasite segments are uncommon but note any anomalies and consult a veterinarian or parasitologist before making diagnoses.

Record environmental conditions (temperature, recent rainfall) as they affect appearance and preservation.

## Habitat / diet clues

Interpreting droppings in context:

- **Insectivorous signals**:
  - Dark, compact pellets with chitin fragments (beetle elytra, ant parts).
  - Often found near feeding sites: under hedges, along garden edges, compost heaps.
- **Omnivorous/fruit signals**:
  - Lighter-colored, softer droppings with visible seeds or fruit pulp.
  - Found near berry patches, hedgerows, or areas with fallen fruit.
- **Mollusc predation**:
  - Shell fragments or slurry-like residues may indicate slug/snail consumption.
- **Human-influenced diet**:
  - Presence of processed food particles, oily residues, or foreign objects may suggest feeding from human-provided sources.
- **Habitat clues**:
  - Proximity to nesting sites (under sheds, piles of leaves) or paths (edges of lawns) can indicate commute corridors.
  - Repeated deposits in a location may signify latrine behavior or a high-use corridor.

Always combine scat evidence with direct observations and other signs (tracks, footprints, nesting material).

## Ethics & safety

Safety and ethics are paramount. Follow these best practices:

- **Do not handle wildlife directly.** Avoid touching hedgehogs or disturbing their nests.
- **Use gloves and tools** (disposable nitrile gloves, sterile spatula) if collecting samples for lab analysis. Label and store samples in appropriate containers.
- **Minimize disturbance.** Photograph from a distance with scale objects (ruler) rather than moving droppings when possible.
- **Hygiene**: Assume potential zoonotic risk. Wash hands thoroughly after fieldwork, disinfect equipment, and avoid contact with eyes/mouth.
- **Legal & welfare considerations**: Check local wildlife protection laws — hedgehogs are protected in some regions. Obtain permits if required for sample collection.
- **Data sensitivity**: When publishing location data of rare or vulnerable populations, consider obfuscating precise coordinates to reduce risk to animals (poaching, disturbance).
- **Reporting anomalies**: If you find dead animals or signs of disease, report to local wildlife rescue or authorities rather than attempting treatment yourself.

These guidelines are for non-clinical fieldwork. For diagnostic work (parasites, pathogens), collaborate with licensed labs under biosafety protocols.

## Future work

Planned directions and open opportunities:

- Standardize a photographic metadata schema (camera settings, scale, GPS, habitat notes).
- Create a citizen-science portal for geo-referenced, anonymized scat records with expert validation.
- Pilot DNA metabarcoding to validate diet inferences from visible contents.
- Train a simple machine-vision classifier to suggest likely diet categories from photos (insect-rich vs fruit-rich vs mixed).
- Develop classroom-friendly lab guides for safe, non-invasive scat examination (for microscopy and ecology lessons).
- Collaborate with local wildlife organizations to integrate non-invasive monitoring into conservation outreach.

Contributions welcome — open issues and PRs are encouraged. Please follow the code of conduct and ethical guidelines when contributing.
